# Spider Biomechanics: A Definitive Quantitative Reference

## TL;DR
- Spiders run on a unique **semi-hydraulic skeleton**: the femur–patella and tibia–metatarsus leg joints have NO extensor muscles and are extended by hemolymph pressure generated in the prosoma (resting ~5–7 kPa; transient peaks to ~60 kPa, and up to ~450 mmHg/~60 kPa during maximal struggle), while flexion is muscular — a design that ties locomotion, circulation and prey capture to one shared pressurized fluid.
- Across every mechanical system spiders exploit **material and structural extremes**: dragline silk (tensile strength ~1–1.5 GPa, toughness ~160–350 MJ/m³, tougher than Kevlar or steel by energy-to-break), zinc-hardened fangs (up to ~23–25% Zn dry weight), hierarchical van-der-Waals foot setae (contact-splitting; ~38 nN per setule), nanometer-sensitive slit-sensilla strain gauges, and power-amplified trap-jaw strikes (chelicerae closing in ~0.12 ms).
- The field rests on identifiable **"titans"** — Parry & Brown (hydraulics), Barth (sensory biomechanics), Gosline/Vollrath/Lewis/Hayashi/Buehler (silk), Arzt & Gorb plus Kesel and Autumn (adhesion), Shultz & Sensenig (elastic energy storage), Prestwich (metabolic constraints), Wood (trap-jaw), and Nabawy (jumping) — and many quantitative questions (true in-vivo role of hydraulics in large spiders, detachment control, spinning physics) remain open.

## Key Findings

1. **Hydraulic leg extension is real but size-dependent.** Parry & Brown (1959) proved leg joints extend hydraulically; modern work (Weihmann et al. 2012; Nabawy et al. 2018) shows large spiders supplement or replace this with flexor-muscle action and that elastic recoil (Sensenig & Shultz 2003) substitutes in related arachnids.
2. **Two of the three main leg joints lack extensors.** The femur–patella and tibia–metatarsus joints are dorsal-rim hinge joints (one rotational DOF each) that muscles can only flex; the hip complex (coxa–trochanter–femur) has both flexors and extensors.
3. **Pressure is shared between locomotion and circulation**, creating a fundamental performance trade-off: during maximal activity prosomal pressure exceeds systolic heart pressure, halting perfusion — a key reason spiders fatigue fast and rely heavily on anaerobic metabolism (Prestwich 1988; Paul et al. 1989).
4. **Adhesion is dry van-der-Waals adhesion via contact splitting**, quantitatively explained by Arzt, Gorb & Spolenak (2003): splitting one contact into n subcontacts multiplies adhesion by √n.
5. **Silk's toughness comes from nanoconfined β-sheet crystals** in an amorphous matrix (Buehler; Keten et al. 2010), with supercontraction governed by water disrupting hydrogen bonds (Gosline; Vollrath).
6. **Jumping spiders combine hydraulics, muscle and a silk dragline tether** and possess depth-perception-from-defocus vision (Nagata et al. 2012).
7. **Power amplification (latch-mediated spring actuation) occurs in trap-jaw spiders** (Wood et al. 2016) and spitting spiders eject venom-silk at up to ~28.8 m/s with fangs oscillating at 278–1781 Hz.

## Details

### 1. Leg Actuation & Hydraulics

**The core principle.** Unlike insects, spiders lack extensor muscles at the two principal leg joints — the femur–patella and tibia–metatarsus joints. These joints are hinge joints whose axes lie at the **dorsal rim** of the segments, so muscles (which pass ventrally) can only flex them. Extension is achieved by raising hemolymph pressure in the prosoma, which flows distally through lacunae (hemolymph-filled spaces) and inflates a bellows-like folded articular membrane at each joint, increasing joint volume and pushing the segments apart (Parry & Brown 1959; Blickhan & Barth 1985; Shultz 1989).

**Pressure generation.** Pressure is produced by **dorsoventral compression of the prosoma**: contraction of dorsoventral muscles (and, per Shultz 1989/1991, endosternal suspensor muscles acting on the mesodermal endosternite) flattens the prosoma, whose soft pleura allow volume to decrease, raising internal hemolymph pressure simultaneously in all legs. Shultz (1991, *J. Morphol.* 210:13–31), studying the giant whipscorpion *Mastigoproctus giganteus*, argued hydraulic extension is primitive in Arachnida and that fluid pressure is generated by endosternal suspensor contraction — though Kropf (2013) cautions this single-species model is contested (e.g., Ricinulei lack contractile endosternal suspensors).

**Measured pressures (key numbers):**
- **Parry & Brown (1959, *J. Exp. Biol.* 36:423–433)**, *Tegenaria atrica*: maintained (resting) pressures ≈ 5 cm Hg (~6.6 kPa); transient pressures up to 40–60 cm Hg (~60 kPa, over half an atmosphere).
- **Stewart & Martin (1974)**, tarantula *Dugesiella hentzi*: maximal-activity peaks reported around 64 kPa — roughly 50× resting values.
- **Paul et al. (1989)**, tarantula *Eurypelma californicum*: prosomal hemolymph pressure in resting restrained animals 41 ± 19 Torr, rising to ~90 Torr during walking and 217 ± 48 Torr during fast sprints; opisthosomal pressures ~20 Torr at rest, 40–60 Torr in locomotion.
- **Prestwich (1988, *J. Comp. Physiol. B* 158:437–447)**, *Filistata*: pressures rose to 100–200 mmHg at onset of activity, peaking at 450 mmHg after ~20 s.
- General consensus values: normal walking 4–8 kPa in legs; intense activity 60 kPa, with reports up to 130 kPa (Göttler et al. 2021).

**Hemolymph as a non-Newtonian fluid.** Recent rheology of jumping-spider hemolymph (Soft Matter 2021) measured apparent viscosity μₐ = 2.7–3.7 cP near zero shear, shear-thinning to 0.6–0.8 cP at wall shear rates ~10⁵ s⁻¹ — comparable to *Manduca sexta* caterpillar hemolymph.

**The locomotion/circulation trade-off & curling at death.** Because the same hemolymph serves both extension and oxygen transport, during maximal activity prosomal pressure exceeds systolic heart pressure and perfusion stops (Paul et al. 1989) — coupling fatigue to the hydraulic demand. At low hemolymph volume or pressure (injury, death, desiccation), extension fails and the legs curl inward under the unopposed pull of the flexor muscles — explaining the classic curled posture of dead/dying spiders. Desiccation and water balance therefore directly constrain locomotor capacity.

**Open questions.** Nabawy et al. (2018) found the regal jumping spider does not *need* hydraulic power for its jumps; co-author Crowther stated "the role of hydraulic movement in spiders remains an open question." The relative contributions of muscle, hydraulics, and elastic recoil in vivo across body sizes are still debated.

### 2. Leg Segment Anatomy & Joint Kinematics

**Seven segments + pretarsus.** Proximal→distal: **coxa, trochanter, femur, patella, tibia, metatarsus, tarsus**, terminating in the pretarsus/claw (Nabawy et al. 2018: "The legs comprise seven segments… The legs terminate with a claw.").

**Degrees of freedom and joint types (Landkammer et al. 2016, *Robotics* 5(3):15; Weihmann et al. 2012):**
- The functional **hip complex** (coxa–trochanter, trochanter–femur) is muscle-actuated with **two degrees of freedom** (stance/levation and pan/promotion-remotion) and has both flexors and extensors; only the trochanterofemoral joint among the jump-critical joints carries extensor muscles.
- The **femur–patella** and **tibia–metatarsus** joints are dorsal-pivot hinge joints with **one rotational DOF each**, muscle-flexed and hydraulically extended.
- Functionally the 7-segment leg reduces to a **three-segment bow** during locomotion: coxa+trochanter (hip), patella+tibia (acting as one), and tarsus (attachment) (Weihmann 2013).

**Measured joint angle ranges:**
- **Booster et al. (2015, *J. Exp. Biol.* 218:977–982)**, tarantula *Aphonopelma hentzi*: working ranges greater in proximal femur–patella joints (50–70°) than distal tibia–metatarsus joints (26–36°); maximum ranges of motion ~160° (femur–patella) and ~125° (tibia–metatarsus) (textbook value via Foelix 2011). Sprint speed increased 2.5× over 15–40°C by changing stride frequency, not stride length.
- **Hao et al. (2019, *Appl. Bionics Biomech.* 2019:4617212)**, tarantula *Grammostola rosea*: femur–patella joint angle varied ~90–130° (smallest range); the other three measured joints varied ~140–170°; the two hydraulically driven joints had the largest excursions.
- **Siebert et al. (2010, *J. Comp. Physiol. B*)**, *Cupiennius salei*: tibia–metatarsus flexor produced 0.07 N·cm maximum isometric moment (25 N/cm² stress) at 160° joint angle.

**Allometry & robotics DOF.** In *Phidippus regius* (Nabawy 2018), legs 1 and 4 are longest (~10.4–10.5 mm), legs 2–3 ~8.4 mm; appendages = 26% of body mass (body 15 mm, 150 mg). For robotic modeling, each leg is treated as a 7-joint manipulator (Gasparetto et al. 2008, ESA/ACT), commonly reduced to 2–3 DOF/leg in engineering practice (≈16–24 DOF for the whole animal; ~56 in the full kinematic model).

### 3. Gaits, Locomotion & Neural Control

**Alternating tetrapod gait.** The candidate primary gait is the alternating tetrapod: two sets of four diagonally adjacent legs (L1,L3,R2,R4 vs L2,L4,R1,R3) move in antiphase — the octopod analogue of the hexapod alternating tripod (Wilson 1967; Spagna & Peattie 2012). Real spiders show high variability and intermediate "metachronal wave" phase relations, especially at speed (Ward & Humphreys 1981; Weihmann 2013).

**Duty factor & coordination.** In *Grammostola rosea* (Hao et al. 2019) duty factor was 0.683 (hard horizontal), 0.668 (soft horizontal), 0.630 (sloped) — always >0.5, indicating static stability. *Cupiennius salei* shows low inter-leg synchrony (synchrony factor ~0.3, Spagna et al. 2011) and small center-of-mass vertical oscillations, which is energetically efficient. Spiders modulate speed by changing stride length and duty factor.

**Fastest spiders.** *Hololena adnexa/curta* (Agelenidae) reach running speeds **occasionally exceeding 70 body-lengths/s (~50 cm/s)**, with the transition to an aerial phase (duty factor <0.5) occurring at an average of 54 body-lengths/s (Spagna, Valdivia & Mohan 2011, *J. Arachnol.* 39:84–91). The Moroccan flic-flac spider *Cebrennus rechenbergi* (Sparassidae; Jäger 2014) escapes by cartwheeling/flic-flac somersaults at up to ~1.7–2 m/s (~100 body-lengths/s), roughly double its running speed, but can only do so a limited number of times before exhaustion. It inspired Rechenberg's "Tabbot" rolling robot.

**Limb loss.** Spiders flexibly adopt modified gaits (even a "tripod"-like pattern) after autotomy; limping can increase stability (Wilshin et al. 2018).

**Neural control.** Locomotion is organized via central pattern generators with sensory feedback from slit sensilla and trichobothria providing reflex correction (Barth 2002); detailed CPG circuitry remains less characterized than in insects.

### 4. Adhesion, Setae & Attachment

**Mechanism: dry van-der-Waals adhesion via hierarchical setae.** Spiders climb glass and walk inverted using scopulae and claw tufts bearing thousands of setae, each split into hundreds of nanoscale spatulate setules. The claw tuft of *Cupiennius salei* comprises ~2,400 setae (Kesel-group work).

**Quantitative adhesion:**
- **Kesel, Martin & Seidl (2003, *J. Exp. Biol.* 206:2733–2738)**, jumping spider *Evarcha arcuata*: a single setule produces an adhesive force F_a ≈ **38.12 nN** perpendicular to a surface. Summed across ~624,000 setules, total F_a ≈ 2.38×10⁻² N versus a 15.1 mg body mass — a **safety factor of ~160** (≈173× body weight in later reckonings).
- Surface-energy dependence (*Cupiennius salei*, Frontiers 2021): adhesion on hydrophobic PTFE ≈ 30% of that on hydrophilic glass; intermediate on epoxy/silicon (~two-thirds of glass) — implicating both van-der-Waals (dispersive) and hydrogen-bond (polar) contributions.

**Contact-splitting theory (Arzt, Gorb & Spolenak 2003, *PNAS* 100:10603):** From JKR contact mechanics, adhesion force scales with a *linear* dimension of contact. Splitting a single contact of radius R into n self-similar subcontacts each of radius R/√n increases total adhesion to:

  **F′_C = √n · F_C**

This explains the inverse scaling: heavier animals have finer, denser terminal contacts (flies: micron-scale; geckos: sub-micron). Terminal element density scales as log N_A ≈ 13.8 + 0.699·log m. Curvature-invariant scaling gives a ⅓ exponent within lineages; self-similarity gives ⅔ across them.

**Comparison to geckos.** Gecko single-seta adhesion is much larger and shear-dependent: 0.6 µN (no sliding) → 13.6 µN (with sliding) → 194 µN at 5 µm sliding/15 µN preload, implying ~100 N per gecko foot (Autumn et al. 2000). Both spiders and geckos use frictional/shear-enhanced van-der-Waals adhesion; spiders are smaller animals with finer per-contact forces. Detachment is achieved by peeling (lifting setules successively rather than all at once), enabling rapid, low-force release; self-cleaning and load-dependent frictional adhesion (alignment of spatulae under shear) are shared themes.

**Tarsal claws** provide mechanical interlock on rough surfaces and silk, complementing the adhesive setae used on smooth substrates.

### 5. Exoskeleton & Cuticle Material Properties

**Composite structure.** Cuticle is a chitin-nanofiber/protein composite. Material property ranges (Vincent & Wegst 2004, *Arthropod Struct. Dev.* 33:187–199):
- Resilin (elastic protein): Young's modulus ≈ 1 MPa
- Soft cuticles: ~1 kPa–50 MPa
- Sclerotized cuticle: 1–20 GPa; Vickers hardness 25–80 kgf/mm²; density ~1–1.3 g/cm³
- Chitin nanofibers: Young's modulus >150 GPa
- Spider exoskeleton effective modulus ~18 GPa (Blickhan & Barth 1985)

**Resilin** was discovered by **Torkel Weis-Fogh (1960, *J. Exp. Biol.* 37:889–907; Andersen & Weis-Fogh 1964)** — a near-perfectly elastic rubber-like protein cross-linked via di-tyrosine, used in joint membranes and energy-storage structures. In arachnids, transarticular sclerites (which may contain resilin) store elastic energy. **Sensenig & Shultz (2003, *J. Exp. Biol.* 206:771–784)** showed certain arachnid leg joints lack extensor muscles but have elastically deformable transarticular sclerites that store energy during muscular flexion and return it as elastic recoil during extension — with **resilience (elastic return efficiency) of 70–90%** for well-developed sclerites. In spiders specifically this function is largely ceded to the hydraulic mechanism, whereas the sclerite-recoil mechanism dominates in harvestmen, solifugids and whip scorpions.

**Metal-hardened fangs (Robert Schofield).** The "tools" of arthropods are hardened with heavy metals and halogens. Schofield's microanalysis (Schofield et al., *Nucl. Instrum. Methods* B; Schofield 2001, 2021 *Sci. Rep.*): the garden spider *Araneus diadematus* has zinc up to ~23% of dry weight in a thin layer near the fang surface, and manganese up to ~5% in the marginal teeth; the wolf spider *Alopecosa kochi* shows similar Zn near the fang surface with Mn ~1.5% beneath; scorpion teeth reach Zn ~24% and Fe ~8%. Zinc cross-links histidine-rich proteins (transported via nano-channels in the fang) to harden the cuticle (Politi et al. 2012). Zn-enriched regions are markedly harder (grasshopper-mandible comparison: Zn-free ~25 kgf/mm² vs Zn-enriched ~80 kgf/mm²). Newly molted tarantula fangs are soft and white, requiring days of zinc incorporation to harden — fang hardening lags ecdysis.

**Molting biomechanics.** During ecdysis the soft new cuticle is hydraulically expanded and then sclerotized; metal incorporation into tools occurs after ecdysis.

### 6. Silk Mechanics

**Hierarchical structure.** Dragline (major ampullate) silk consists of β-sheet nanocrystals (poly-Ala / poly-(Gly-Ala), from MaSp1/MaSp2 spidroins) embedded in a semi-amorphous matrix of 3₁-helices and β-turns. The nanocrystals are densely hydrogen-bonded.

**Mechanical properties:**
- Tensile strength ~0.88–1.5 GPa (some report up to 4.4–4.8 GPa for *Nephila clavipes* MA silk; minor ampullate 0.91–1.1 GPa)
- Extensibility ~18–35% (MA); flagelliform capture silk up to ~300%
- Toughness (energy to break) ~160 MJ/m³ for *Nephila*, exceeding steel and Kevlar by energy-to-break; jump-spun salticid silk mean ~282 MJ/m³
- Young's modulus on the order of ~10 GPa (dry MA)

**Buehler's molecular insight (Keten, Xu, Ihle & Buehler 2010, *Nature Materials* 9:359–367; Nova et al. 2010, *Nano Letters* 10:2626):** Counterintuitively, silk's strength derives from weak hydrogen bonds because β-sheet nanocrystals confined to a few nanometers achieve maximal stiffness, strength and toughness — small crystals load H-bonds in uniform *shear* (strong) rather than non-uniform *tension* (weak). Shear modulus ~2.38 GPa (*N. clavipes*).

**Supercontraction (Work 1977; Gosline; Vollrath):** On wetting (humidity >~70%), MA silk absorbs water that disrupts H-bonds in non-crystalline regions, allowing them to recoil to higher-entropy configurations; the fiber shrinks up to ~50% and behaves like an elastomer. Strength of supercontraction correlates with proline content and GPGGX/GGX motif abundance.

**Silk types & glands.** Orb-weavers spin up to seven silks: **major ampullate** (dragline, frame/radii), **minor ampullate** (auxiliary spiral), **flagelliform** (capture-spiral core, highly extensible), **aggregate** (glue), **tubuliform/cylindrical** (egg case), **aciniform** (wrapping), **piriform** (attachment discs).

**Web energy absorption.** Frame/radial MA silk and elastic flagelliform silk together arrest and dissipate prey kinetic energy. A 120 mg honeybee at ~3.1 m/s carries substantial kinetic energy that the web's combination of strength and extensibility dissipates without recoiling the prey out — orb webs are tuned shock absorbers (Blackledge; Vollrath).

**Spinning.** Silk is spun from a liquid-crystalline dope via a pH/ion/shear-driven transition in the spinning duct. Jumping spiders spin dragline mid-jump at ~500–700 mm/s (100–140 body-lengths/s) yet maintain high quality — far faster than the ~2–20 mm/s that degrades other spiders' silk (Current Biology 2021).

### 7. Jumping Spider (Salticidae) Specializations

**Ballistic leap (Nabawy, Sivalingam, Garwood, Crowther & Sellers 2018, *Sci. Rep.* 8:7142).** The trained regal jumping spider "Kim" (*Phidippus regius*, 150 mg, 15 mm) was filmed at 3,200 fps over 15 jump tasks:
- Take-off velocity 0.52–0.97 m/s (mean ~0.95 m/s)
- Acceleration **1.68–5.43 g** (mean g-force ~4.7); the force on the legs at take-off reaches up to ~5× the spider's body weight
- Take-off angle −10.5° to 53.5°
- Time to take-off ~18–32 ms (mean 33.31 ± 3.65 ms)
- Jump distance up to ~5 body lengths (descending), ~4 (level), ~3 (ascending); salticids can leap up to ~6 body lengths / 160 mm (vs ~1.5 body lengths for humans)
- Leg extension during acceleration is primarily straightening at the femur–patella hinge; both 3rd and 4th legs power the jump
- Conclusion: *P. regius* uses dynamic muscle contraction (not a catapult); "hydraulic augmentation may be present but appears not to be energetically essential." For short close jumps it uses faster, energy-costly low trajectories (accuracy); for longer/higher jumps it minimizes energy.

**Dragline tether.** Salticids pay out a dragline during jumps. Chen et al. (2013, *J. R. Soc. Interface*, *Hasarius adansoni*) showed the dragline stabilizes mid-air attitude: spiders pitch backward after takeoff, then use an internal friction brake on the dragline to reverse pitch and land parallel; cutting the dragline causes clumsy, tumbling landings. This inspired tethered-attitude-control robots (Cape Town's LEAP; UCLA's SPLITTER). Hill's work on *Phidippus* details the dragline "catching basket" mechanics in prey capture.

**Vision (Nagata et al. 2012, *Science* 335:469–471).** Principal (anterior median) eyes have a unique retina with **four tiered photoreceptor layers**. Because the lens has chromatic aberration, green light is focused on the deepest layer while the second-deepest green-sensitive layer always receives a *defocused* image — the spider judges depth from the degree of defocus (depth-from-defocus), the only animal known to use this as its primary depth cue. Confirmed behaviorally: under green light spiders jump short. The principal eyes have boom-mounted movable retinae for scanning, layered telephoto optics, and color vision (receptors at ~360 nm UV, ~480–500, ~520–540, ~580 nm); secondary eyes (anterior/posterior lateral) give wide-field motion detection and a near-360° visual field. This sophisticated processing occurs in a tiny brain.

**Hearing at a distance.** Beyond vision, salticids detect airborne sound far further than long assumed: in *Phidippus audax*, auditory neurons respond even when the sound source is ~3 m away (~600 body lengths) at amplitudes ~65 dB SPL, and 80 Hz tones trigger a freeze startle response (Shamble et al. 2016, *Current Biology* 26:2913–2920) — mediated largely by trichobothria.

### 8. Respiration, Circulation & Physiological Limits

**Respiratory organs.** Most spiders have one pair of anterior book lungs plus a posterior tracheal system; basal lineages have two pairs of book lungs. Book lungs are stacks of lamellae ("pages") maximizing diffusion surface; tracheae deliver O₂ directly to tissues. In the jumping spider *Salticus scenicus*, tracheae make up 25–35% of total diffusing capacity (Schmitz & Perry 2001). Tracheal development trades off inversely with book-lung area (Opell; uloborid study).

**Open circulation.** A dorsal tubular heart pumps hemocyanin-carrying hemolymph into sinuses; resting heart rate in *Eurypelma* ~21 bpm, rising to ~90 bpm after burst activity (Paul et al. 1989).

**The pressure trade-off and fatigue.** Aerobic capacity correlates with book-lung surface area; aerobic scope is only ~3–8× (lower than insects) (Prestwich 1983; Anderson & Prestwich 1982). During maximal struggles, 55–94% of power is anaerobic (Prestwich 1983), and prosomal lactate reaches vertebrate-fatigue levels after ~1 minute. Because pressurizing the hemolymph for leg extension can exceed systolic heart pressure, perfusion is interrupted during peak effort — large spiders therefore cannot sustain running and fatigue rapidly. This is the central physiological constraint: the same fluid is simultaneously hydraulic actuator and oxygen-transport medium.

### 9. Predation & Strike Mechanics

**Fangs and venom.** Chelicerae bear hollow fangs (zinc-hardened tips, §5) that inject venom; the prosomal pressure system also assists cheliceral movement (Wood & Shultz 2025).

**Trap-jaw spiders (Wood et al. 2016, *Current Biology* 26:1057; Wood 2020, *J. Exp. Biol.* 223:jeb219899).** Hannah Wood (Smithsonian) filmed Mecysmaucheniidae (New Zealand/southern South America) at up to 40,000 fps: the fastest species snap their chelicerae shut in **~0.12 ms**, with strikes spanning 0.12–5.6 ms across species (the fastest species being ~two orders of magnitude faster than the slowest) and peak appendage speeds >18.2 m/s. The movements exceed direct muscle power output, indicating **power amplification via a latch-mediated spring (LaMSA)** mechanism; this evolved independently at least **four times** within the family (convergent evolution). µCT revealed a modified carapace orienting cheliceral muscles horizontally.

**Spitting spiders (Suter & Stratton 2009, *J. Insect Sci.* 9:62).** *Scytodes thoracica* ejects a venom-laced silk-glue mixture from fangs through a ~14 µm orifice in <35 ms (full scan of 5–57 fang cycles typically <30 ms; mean overall 25 ms), with fangs oscillating at **278–1781 Hz** producing a zigzag pattern; ejection velocities up to **28.8 m/s**. The spit is contractile, shortening 40–60% and exerting 0.1–0.3 mN to immobilize prey at a distance. The tiny fangs (allometric neoteny) are required for such high-frequency oscillation.

### 10. Sensory Biomechanics (Friedrich Barth's domain)

**Slit sensilla & lyriform organs.** Unique to arachnids, slit sensilla are slits in the cuticle (length ~8–200 µm, width 1–2 µm), each innervated by two sensory cells, that respond to cuticular strain from muscle activity, hemolymph pressure, and substrate vibration. *Cupiennius salei* has ~3,300 slits, ~86% on the legs/pedipalps (Barth & Libera 1970). Grouped in parallel arrays, they form **lyriform organs** (strain gauges). Sensitivity is extreme: slit compression of as little as ~1.5 nm (strains down to ~−10 µε) triggers action potentials. The covering membrane has elastic moduli ~150–500 MPa (AFM; Young et al.). The metatarsal lyriform organ HS10 (21 slits) is a vibration detector; at high frequencies it responds to tarsal deflections as small as 0.05° (~4.5 nm). Barth's book *A Spider's World: Senses and Behavior* (2002) is the standard reference.

**Trichobothria.** Fine, highly compliant filiform hairs detect air movement/vibration over ~10–1000 Hz with sensitivity approaching thermal-noise energy levels (Barth & Höller 1999; Barth 2004). They enable detection of flying prey and airborne stimuli (and, with the principal eyes' processing, underlie distant sound detection in salticids).

**Tactile hairs and tip-pore (chemosensory) sensilla** complete the mechanosensory/chemosensory array. Slit sensilla also contribute to proprioception and path integration.

## Recommendations (for researchers/engineers building on this)

**Stage 1 — Adopt the validated principles now.** Several spider principles are well-understood and engineering-ready:
- *Contact-splitting adhesives*: use the √n scaling and sub-micron fibrillar terminal elements for dry, reusable, residue-free adhesives (Gorb/Arzt). Benchmark against ~38 nN/setule and per-animal safety factors of ~160.
- *Silk-inspired materials*: target nanoconfined β-sheet crystals (<few nm) in an amorphous matrix (Buehler) for toughness >150 MJ/m³; exploit recombinant spidroins (Lewis, Hayashi).
- *Semi-hydraulic / fluidic actuators*: replicate the bellows-folded articular membrane and flexor-only antagonism for lightweight compliant joints (Landkammer; Göttler et al. 2021).
- *Slit-sensilla strain sensors*: nanometer-scale crack-based strain gauges (already commercialized as ultrasensitive flexible sensors inspired by lyriform organs).

**Stage 2 — Resolve the contested mechanics before scaling.** Do NOT assume pure hydraulics drives large-spider locomotion; integrate muscle and elastic recoil. Use in-vivo pressure + EMG + high-speed 3D kinematics (Weihmann method) across a body-size series to map the muscle/hydraulic/elastic contribution. Threshold to change approach: if measured ground-reaction force vectors pass joints dorsally (as in *Ancylometes*), favor a flexor-dominant model.

**Stage 3 — Pursue the open frontiers.** Depth-from-defocus monocular sensors (ESA studied this), dragline-tether attitude control for jumping robots (LEAP/SPLITTER), and LaMSA power-amplified micro-mechanisms (trap-jaw) are promising but immature. Benchmark jumping robots against Kim's 5.43 g peak acceleration and ~18–32 ms takeoff; benchmark fast-running/rolling robots against flic-flac's ~100 body-lengths/s and *Hololena*'s >70 body-lengths/s.

## Caveats

- **Hydraulics vs. muscle is unresolved**: classic teaching overstates pure hydraulic drive; in large spiders flexor muscles and (in related arachnids) elastic transarticular sclerites contribute substantially. Nabawy's jumping data and Weihmann's running data both qualify the hydraulic narrative.
- **The endosternite pump model (Shultz 1991)** rests largely on one whipscorpion species and is contested (Kropf 2013); pressure generation likely involves multiple muscle groups.
- **Adhesion detachment control and self-cleaning** are qualitatively described but not fully quantified; whole-animal safety factors assume all setules contact simultaneously, which is unrealistic in vivo.
- **Some popular-press figures are unreliable** (e.g., the absurd "1,692 mph" triangle-weaver claim circulating online conflates web-release acceleration with body speed); the verified fastest *running/rolling* spider is *Cebrennus rechenbergi* at ~1.7–2 m/s, and the fastest *strike* is the trap-jaw at ~0.12 ms.
- **"~5× body weight" jump force** is a derived approximation from Nabawy's measured 5.43 g peak acceleration, not a verbatim force-to-weight ratio in that paper.
- **Silk strength values vary widely** (0.88–4.8 GPa) by species, hydration, strain rate and reeling speed; always cite measurement conditions.
- **CPG circuitry in spiders** is far less characterized than in insects; neural-control claims are largely inferred from behavior and reflex studies rather than mapped circuits.
